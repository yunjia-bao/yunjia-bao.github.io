Special Focus: Witnessing the Human History, Seeing the Black Hole

Press conference playback from NSF
(Other press conferences can be found here)

Here is the first image of the event horizon of a black hole in human history from the supermassive black hole at the center of M87. Credit: Event Horizon Telescope Collaboration

Published papers on this study by the ETH collaboration (the series of six papers, First M87 Event Horizon Telescope Results, on Astrophysical Journal Letters): [Ap.J.Lett. 1], [Ap.J.Lett. 2], [Ap.J.Lett. 3], [Ap.J.Lett. 4], [Ap.J.Lett. 5], [Ap.J.Lett. 6].

The proposal of the existence of a massive gravitating body, a black hole, is more and more accepted by physicists due to recent observations of gravitational wave signals (GW) and the tracking of celestial bodies near SgrA*, the proposed supermassive black hole at the center of the Milky Way. However, direct observation of a black hole had never been performed and released to the public until 10 April 2019. The Event Horizon Telescope (EHT), an international collaboration of Earth-based telescopes using interferometry to observe SgrA*, has finally released its first direct observation of the black hole at the center of M87.

Although Einstein's general relativity describes almost perfectly the classical (as opposed to quantum) gravitation, its puzzling incompatibility with semi-classical field theory and known (second) quantization schemes leads to the interest in studying a quantum theory of gravity. Some interesting proposals include dimensional reduction near the event horizon [arXiv PDF], the holographic principle [arXiv PDF], and the black hole firewall [arXiv PDF]. The strong gravitational interaction results in significant deformation of the spacetime manifold, leading to some paradoxical phenomena such as information loss. An in-depth observation of a black hole will provide many fresh perspectives on where to advance for a more precise quantum theory of gravity.

LIGO's observations on GW provides some interesting results related to some theoretical proposals, e.g. [Echoes from the Abyss arXiv PDF]. However, we have no direct image of any black holes, therefore knowing very little about their electromagnetic properties and structures. EHT's release is groundbreaking as it was the first time ever, in thousands of years of human history, for human beings to successfully observe a black hole directly. This freshly-invented sets of observation tools and techniques will revolutionize the study of extreme gravity, quantum gravity, cosmology, quantum field theory, astrophysics, etc. Also, its first set of papers triumphantly confirmed once again Einstein's general relativity. Curiously, the analysis agrees with the Kerr model, i.e. a rotating black hole, so can we extract the electric charge of the black hole from EHT's data? Can we resolve some dynamics of the photons near the event horizon? Is there anything in the observation that deviates from general relativity?
