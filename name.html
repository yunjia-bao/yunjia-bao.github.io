How do I pronounce your name?

Thanks for asking. I know that Chinese can be a challenging language to learn, and pronouncing a bizarre-looking romanization can be mildly frustrating. First, don't feel nervous. I totally understand it since I unintentionally mispronounce English words/names all the time. Simply try it. I should recognize your pronunciation fairly quickly.

Chinese name, like some other East Asian languages, such as Japanese, Korean, etc., places the surname before the given name. So in Chinese, my name is pronounced (and also written) as "Bao, Yunjia". But I accept both orderings, and it is convenient to use the English ordering to avoid confusions. For relatively close friends and colleagues, I welcome you to simply address me with my given name. In fact, I find calling only my given name by my friends quite a delightful experience.

Unlike many languages, Chinese is a tonal language, i.e. pitch and intonation are part of its phonology. This "tone" is an extra dimension other than vowels and consonants to distinguish words. I am also aware that this can be tricky. Don't be afraid. I understand this. For my name, try your best to reproduce the tone or simply ignore it. It's fine.

Why does your name sound so difficult?

Trust me. You are not the only one who thinks that. It is rather a coincidence that my name contains two non-existing phonemes in English: the vowel, ü or /y/, and the consonant, j or /t͡ɕ/. This is why my name not only looks confusing but also sounds strange.


However, the first vowel is quite prevalent in many languages with subtle but phonetically indistinguishable differences. For English speakers, you may imagine this sound as a muffled combination of i as in pick and u as in true. But in reality, it is really one vowel sound.


The consonant is a little exotic but is still used by several languages. For English speakers, you can imagine it as a consonant in between j as in jump and z as in zero (and if you wish, with a little y as in yes). Again, this is just a consonant on its own instead of a mesh of all of aforementioned phonemes, but mixing them and finding the middle ground is a good starting point.
